Treasury Curve Event Study

Event Context

Event date: 2022-06-15. Pre-event curve date: 2022-06-14. Post-event curve date: 2022-06-16. After the meeting, the federal funds target range was 1.50% to 1.75%.

The Treasury curve is a liquid USD government benchmark and an approximate risk-free curve for rates-risk visualization. In practice, banks often use SOFR/OIS curves for collateralized derivatives discounting, while Treasury curves are widely used for government-bond pricing, relative-value analysis, key-rate risk, and spread measurement.

Which Curve Is This?

The plotted event-study curve is the fitted zero curve when private on-the-run Treasury prices are available locally. The fitted zero rates are derived from selected on-the-run issue dirty prices, where dirty price equals TDNOMPRC + TDACCINT. The observed on-the-run YTM curve is also built for diagnostics from TDYLD. On public GitHub Pages, where private WRDS/CRSP data are unavailable, the site uses deterministic fallback curves so the page remains reproducible without publishing raw data.

This is not the official Treasury par yield curve unless the public government benchmark data are explicitly shown in the comparison chart. It is also not a SOFR/OIS discount curve used by banks for collateralized derivatives.

Treasury yields are not perfectly pure risk-free rates. They may include liquidity premium, term premium, safe-haven demand, inflation expectations, supply/demand effects, and tax or regulatory effects. On-the-run Treasuries are very liquid benchmark securities, but they can trade rich because of liquidity and specialness, lowering their yields relative to other benchmark curves.

Curve Methodology Comparison

Curve type Built from Meaning Used for in this project
Treasury par yield curve Official government par yield data. Coupon rate that would price a hypothetical Treasury at par for each maturity. Public benchmark comparison.
On-the-run observed YTM curve WRDS/CRSP on-the-run Treasury issue yields or prices. Yield to maturity of the most liquid benchmark issues. Event-study visualization and benchmark curve movement.
Fitted zero curve Dirty prices and cashflows. Discount rates by maturity that approximately reprice selected Treasury issues. Pricing, DV01, forward-rate calculation, and P&L explain.
SOFR/OIS curve Overnight indexed swaps / SOFR-linked instruments. Secured overnight collateralized discount curve. Future extension for realistic derivatives discounting.

Why Can This Differ From The Government Treasury Curve?

  1. The Treasury par yield curve is a par curve; WRDS TDYLD is individual issue yield to maturity.
  2. Fitted zero rates are not YTMs; they are discount rates by maturity.
  3. On-the-run bonds can trade rich because of liquidity and special repo demand, lowering their yields.
  4. Government par curves are smoothed/interpolated benchmark curves; individual issue curves are based on specific securities.
  5. Timing and quote source may differ. WRDS/CRSP fields include TDSOURCR and price flags.
  6. Clean price vs dirty price matters. Cashflow pricing should use dirty price = TDNOMPRC + TDACCINT.
  7. Bills, notes, and bonds can use different yield conventions.
  8. Column mapping errors can happen if maturities are inferred by position instead of explicit column names.

If the on-the-run curve differs from the government curve by unusually large amounts, check date filtering, maturity mapping, percent vs decimal scaling, clean vs dirty price, coupon and maturity fields, TIPS/callable filtering, and whether fallback synthetic data were used.

Desk-Quant Interpretation

The case study frames the meeting as a rates-risk shock rather than a single-price event. The pre/post curve chart shows how the curve level changed, while the basis-point move chart separates parallel from non-parallel behavior. The 3D surfaces show whether the event shift was isolated to one date or part of a broader June curve repricing.

For desk risk, the non-parallel pattern matters because a portfolio with concentrated 2Y, 5Y, 10Y, or 30Y key-rate exposure can gain or lose even when the headline level move looks modest. The forward surface adds another lens by showing how the event changed implied rates between maturity buckets.
