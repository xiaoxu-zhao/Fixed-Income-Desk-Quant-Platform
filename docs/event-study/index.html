Curve scope

On-the-run Treasury benchmark

The on-the-run Treasury curve is used as a liquid USD government benchmark and approximate risk-free curve for rates-risk visualization. It is not the same as a SOFR/OIS derivatives discount curve.

Curve terminology

Which curve is this?

The plotted curve is either the on-the-run observed YTM curve or the fitted zero curve, depending on available inputs. It is not the official Treasury par yield curve unless the public government benchmark series is shown separately.

Interpretation

Why non-parallel shifts matter

A flat curve move would imply a parallel shift. Uneven maturity-by-maturity moves imply a non-parallel shift, which matters because a portfolio can be hedged against parallel DV01 but still lose money from slope or curvature exposure.

Forward rates help show how implied future rate expectations changed across maturity buckets, not just how spot curve points moved.

Curve terminology

What this is not

This is not a live trading system, not the official Treasury par yield curve unless a public government benchmark file is supplied, and not a SOFR/OIS discount curve used for collateralized derivatives.

Why can this differ from the government Treasury curve?
  1. Treasury par yield is a par curve; WRDS TDYLD is individual issue yield to maturity.
  2. Fitted zero rates are not YTMs; they are discount rates by maturity.
  3. On-the-run bonds can trade rich because of liquidity and special repo demand.
  4. Government par curves are smoothed/interpolated benchmark curves; individual issue curves use specific securities.
  5. Timing, quote source, price flags, clean versus dirty price, yield conventions, and explicit maturity mapping all matter.

If differences are unusually large, check date filtering, maturity mapping, percent versus decimal scaling, coupon and maturity fields, TIPS/callable filtering, and whether fallback synthetic data were used.
